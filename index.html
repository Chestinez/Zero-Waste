At the heart of every zero-waste lifestyle are five core principles: Refuse, Reduce, Reuse, Recycle, and Compost. Hover over each info card below to explore these practices interactively!

Refuse

Refusing means saying no to items we don't truly need, especially single-use plastics like straws, disposable cutlery, extra bags, or promotional freebies. Refusing helps reduce unnecessary waste before it even enters our homes and sends a clear message to companies that consumers want more sustainable options.

Reuse

Reusing means finding new life in old items. Instead of discarding them, repurpose or donate what you no longer need. Bring your own bottle, repair broken goods, or upcycle creatively. Reusing extends a product's life and reduces waste in landfills.

Reduce

Reducing is about minimizing what we consume. Choose higher-quality, longer-lasting items over disposable ones. Avoid excessive packaging and conserve energy and water. By consuming less, we waste less‚Äîand help preserve our planet's resources.

Recycle

Recycling turns used materials into new products, conserving raw resources and cutting pollution. However, not all materials are recyclable. Understand your local recycling rules, and always clean and sort properly‚Äîespecially when using Singapore's blue bins. Recycling is important, but it should come after refusing, reducing, and reusing.

Compost

Composting transforms food scraps and organic waste into nutrient-rich soil. Instead of sending peels and leftovers to landfills where they emit harmful gases, composting lets you enrich gardens or plants‚Äîeven in a small bin at home!

By eliminating take away packaging, especially materials that are non biodegradable, like plastic bags and styrofoam containers. üè° Kamikatsu

üè° Kamikatsu (Japan)

Kamikatsu is a town in Japan with a bold goal: Zero Waste by 2025. Though having such a daring quest, residents have shown successful progress in reducing their waste significantly. They achieved this by separating their trash into 45 categories to recycle and reuse nearly everything. Their efforts even stretch to no garbage collection or disposing within the town and even a free store where residents either take or donate usable second-hand items called the "Kuru Kuru shop". Their success proves that community-wide efforts can work!

üöó Subaru

üöó Subaru

Subaru's factory in Indiana is the first auto plant in the U.S. to achieve zero-landfill status. This means all manufacturing waste is either recycled, reused, or converted to energy and none is sent to landfills. For example, paint sludge is dried and repurposed into products like parking lot bumpers, and brass lug nuts used during shipping are returned to suppliers for reuse until worn out . This is not only overall beneficial for the entirety of thw world's Zero Waste pursuit, but an inspiration to even future or even current "big" named brands to test their own waters as well.
